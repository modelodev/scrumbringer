# Product

## Register

product

## Users

ScrumBringer is for agile software teams of roughly 5-20 people who want less push-style task assignment and more self-directed flow. The primary users are developers choosing work from a shared pool, tech leads creating and shaping tasks, and scrum masters or project managers monitoring whether the pool is healthy.

Users are usually in an operational context: scanning available work, deciding what to claim, understanding what is already in progress, and keeping the team moving without turning the tool into a micromanagement layer.

## Product Purpose

ScrumBringer is a pull-based agile task manager built around a shared pool and self-assignment. Tasks are made visible, prioritized, filtered by capability, claimed by the person doing the work, released when needed, and completed when done.

The product exists to replace direct assignment bottlenecks with visible flow and personal ownership. Success means the team can see what needs attention, developers can confidently choose the next task, and leads can understand delivery health through signals like time to first claim, pool flow ratio, release rate, active work, priority, and aging.

## Brand Personality

The product personality is autonomous, clear, and operational.

ScrumBringer should feel like a working cockpit for a team that trusts its members: direct, practical, calm under load, and precise about state. The voice should make responsibility visible without sounding punitive, and it should favor short task-oriented language over ceremony.

## Anti-references

ScrumBringer should not feel like Jira, Asana, or Trello when those tools reinforce direct assignment, backlog sprawl, and manager-driven routing.

Avoid:

- Push-assignment patterns that imply someone else owns deciding who does the work.
- Static backlog views where old and urgent work look the same.
- Decorative dashboards that hide the next action.
- Process-heavy UI that turns rules, workflows, or cards into paperwork.
- Ambiguous ownership states such as "assigned", "claimed", and "now working" blending together.
- Visual noise that makes the shared pool harder to scan.

## Design Principles

1. Make pull behavior obvious.
   The interface should always make it clear what is available, what I have claimed, what I am actively working on, and what can be released or completed.

2. Preserve team autonomy.
   UI flows should support choosing, claiming, and returning work without implying a manager is manually assigning tasks.

3. Keep flow health visible.
   Priority, age, blockers, claim activity, release activity, and active work should surface as operational signals, not as decorative metrics.

4. Favor short, decisive interactions.
   Developers should be able to scan, filter, claim, start, pause, complete, and release work without navigating through heavy forms or modal-first flows.

5. Let rules stay behind the work.
   Workflows and automation matter, but the primary surface should keep tasks, cards, capabilities, and current work understandable at a glance.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No formal accessibility target has been set for this project yet.

Future interface work should still preserve basic product usability: readable text, visible focus states, keyboard-accessible controls, non-color-only status communication, and reduced-motion alternatives where motion is introduced.
